Large language models (LLMs) provide new possibilities for accelerating materials research, yet their application in complex materials science remains limited. Here, we developed the
collaborative multi-agent and robot system (MARS), a knowledge-driven hierarchical architecture coordinating 19 LLM agents with 16 domain-specific tools organized into functional modules, achieving closedloop autonomous materials discovery by integrating robotic experimentation. MARS offers accurate and professional guidance for materials development and relieves the hallucination inherent to current LLMs through
a hybrid retrieval-augmented generation approach. As experimental validation, the system demonstrates its
capabilities in perovskite nanocrystal development, optimizing synthesis protocols within 10 experimental
iterations and designing a biomimetic ‘‘core-shell-corona’’ structure for water-stable perovskite composites
within 3.5 h compared to conventional methods requiring 4–6 months. Through an interactive natural language interface, researchers can orchestrate experiments while tracking real-time agent coordination.
Beyond efficiency gains, MARS represents a paradigm shift toward truly autonomous materials research
where knowledge-driven AI can initiate scientific discovery. MARS can combine knowledge from different
scientific fields to create solutions that human researchers might overlook, as demonstrated by its biomimetic design inspired by cell membranes. Looking forward, we envision MARS accelerating discoveries
across critical areas including renewable energy materials, medical compounds, and sustainable technologies. This could help address urgent global challenges like climate change and healthcare needs by removing
research bottlenecks. As the system evolves, it could democratize advanced materials research by reducing
dependence on specialized infrastructure and expertise, allowing scientists worldwide to focus on creative
problem-solving rather than repetitive tasks. MARS represents not just an improvement in research efficiency but also a fundamental shift in how we approach materials innovation.

INTRODUCTION
Materials are the foundation of human understanding and transformation of the world, and innovations in this field are critical to
societal progress and technological advancement.1,2 The discovery and application of new materials is typically a complex,
lengthy, and costly process that requires the accumulation of
multidisciplinary knowledge, extensive study of prior research,
expertise in preparation techniques, time-intensive experimental
iterations, and thorough validation in practical scenarios.3–6 To
enhance the efficiency of materials innovation, scientists have
made sustained and long-term efforts, such as the launch of
the Materials Genome Initiative in 2011, which aims to accelerate
materials discovery using high-throughput computational and
experimental platforms.7–9 Despite these advances, the development of new materials typically still requires years, highlighting
the urgent need for new methodologies to accelerate the pace of
materials research.
Since OpenAI released ChatGPT, large language models
(LLMs) have attracted widespread research interest, leading to
substantial breakthroughs across various fields.10,11 LLMs can
serve as invaluable research assistants and aid researchers in efficiently mining literature, quickly accessing critical data, and
providing potential optimization strategies.12–15 Besides, LLMs
have recently shown exceptional abilities in code generation,
specialized tool integration, and strategic planning.16–23 These advances have enabled several pioneering studies that successfully
couple LLM-powered agents with robotic experimentation platforms, achieving autonomous chemical synthesis,24,25 accelerated reaction optimization,26 and intelligent synthesis route
recommendation,27 thereby demonstrating the transformative
potential of AI-driven laboratory automation. These capabilities
are particularly valuable for materials science, where the inherently multidisciplinary nature demands precise knowledge extraction and integration from an ever-expanding scientific literature.28
This is particularly evident in nanomaterials research, a field characterized by an explosion of scientific literature over the past two
decades, yet struggles with challenges of research redundancy
and reproducibility.29,30 While promising, leveraging the full
potential of LLMs for autonomous materials research requires addressing key challenges, including accurate comprehension of
domain-specific knowledge, mitigation of hallucinations, robust
control of automated synthesis, efficient management of complex
tasks, and development of systematic workflows.
Herein, we develop a knowledge-driven multi-agent and robot
system (MARS) for end-to-end autonomous materials discovery.
MARS employs a collaborative network of 19 specialized LLM
agents with access to 16 domain-specific tools to orchestrate
complex material research tasks by integrating the robotic plat-

The architecture of MARS
This automated materials research framework employs a multiagent system architecture that is analogous to a well-coordinated research laboratory. The MARS framework coordinates
a network of 19 specialized agents, enabling complex multiobjective tasks in materials design, synthesis, and analysis
through collaborative intelligence. These agents constitute a
higher-level framework built on LLMs, leveraging their core
reasoning and generative capabilities while incorporating task
definition, dynamic utilization of 16 professional toolkits, and
feedback loops to achieve complex objectives beyond the capacity of standalone LLMs. As shown in Figure 1A, the system
is hierarchically structured into five synergistic functional groups
with distinct responsibilities to emulate the real-world scientific
research team, including the ORCHESTRATOR group (G1),
SCIENTIST group (G2), ENGINEER group (G3), EXECUTOR
group (G4), and ANALYST group (G5) (see Note S1).
The ORCHESTRATOR Group serves as the central coordinator, plans sub-tasks based on user requests, and selects the
appropriate agent group to complete the sub-task, while

Knowledge-driven automated experimentation
Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) enhances language
model outputs by incorporating relevant information from
external knowledge sources before generating responses. This
approach grounds model outputs in factual, domain-specific information, thereby reducing hallucinations that commonly occur
when models rely exclusively on knowledge embedded during
training.32 MARS integrates materials knowledge retrieval with
experimental execution, forming complementary cognitive and
physical dimensions that enable the system to transition from
conceptual understanding to real-world synthesis. Research
on materials typically begins with comprehensive literature review and information retrieval. The SCIENTIST group accelerates this process through a hybrid RAG approach that transforms unstructured scientific literature into structured
experimental guidance. Our hybrid RAG framework combines
traditional vector similarity search with graph-based retrieval
methods (GraphRAG), enabling more comprehensive and contextually aware information retrieval for complex materials science queries.
As illustrated in Figure 2A, four specialized LLM-based agents
within the SCIENTIST group focus on distinct areas: structure,
synthesis, properties, and applications, each contributing
distinct expertise while leveraging specialized retrieval tools.
The core retrieval system combines three primary approaches:
KeywordSearch for word-level query matching, VectorSearch
for semantic-level similarity-based matching, and GraphRAG
for topic-level view through the knowledge graph. This hybrid
approach ensures comprehensive query results by balancing
sentence matching, paragraph matching, and topic view.
As shown in Figure 2B, MARS utilizes an extensive knowledge
base carefully constructed from academic papers, books, and
web resources to support this retrieval system (see Note S3).
These diverse sources are converted to markdown format for
natural segmentation and then enhanced with vector representations for similarity searches and structured into knowledge
graphs for conceptual reasoning.33–35 Online resources
(WebSearch, LitSearch, and DatabaseSearch) supplement the
offline knowledge base when required. Upon receiving a subtask from the ORCHESTRATOR, the admin agent of the
SCIENTIST group routes it to the most appropriate specialized
agent using tailored prompts. These specialized agents utilize
hybrid retrieval tools to acquire relevant domain knowledge
when needed, thus enhancing the accuracy of their responses.
To demonstrate the capabilities of MARS, the synthesis of
perovskite nanocubes is selected as a representative task.
CsPbBr3 perovskites are promising for optoelectronic applications, but their nanocrystal synthesis is complex, with multiple
interdependent factors including precursor selection, ligand engineering, reaction temperature control, and precise timing of
various steps. For this demonstration, a specialized knowledge
base was constructed from 1,348 high-impact perovskite publications, resulting in a vector database (7,818 segments) and a
comprehensive knowledge graph (117,749 nodes and 232,490
edges), which provides a rich foundation for material synthesis

Figure 2E illustrates the ENGINEER group, which serves as the

the structural engineer converts protocols to standardized JSON

Figure 2F demonstrates this workflow through the automated
room temperature synthesis of CsPbBr3 nanocrystals. Upon
receiving the synthesis scheme from the SCIENTIST group, the
ENGINEER group transforms the protocol into machine-executable instructions, which are subsequently passed to the
EXECUTOR group. The workstation and mobile-robot controller
agents transmit these instructions through the scheme transmission API tool to the unified control middleware on the computer
workstation, thereby initiating the physical synthesis process.
The middleware coordinates a sequence of automated operations: (1) mobile robots locate reagents and consumables from
storage areas (no. 1–3); (2) the robots transport reagent bottles
to the adding device (no. 4–5); (3) the materials are transferred
to the liquid workstation (no. 6) for precise pipetting, stirring,
and heating; and (4) the completed samples are transported to
the fluorescence spectrometer (no. 7) for characterization.
Throughout this process, spectroscopic data are transmitted
to the middleware and accessed by the DataCollector agent
for analysis.

Knowledge-driven materials design
The design of novel functional materials typically requires researchers to possess extensive cross-disciplinary knowledge
and rich experiential accumulation, particularly for complex material systems that necessitate balancing multiple performance
dimensions. Figure 4 demonstrates in detail how the MARS system overcomes limitations of traditional development paradigms
to assist scientists in achieving materials design and synthesis
validation of perovskite ternary nanocomposite structures (see
Note S8). As shown in Figure 4A, traditional materials design cycles typically require several months, encompassing literature
review, experimental design, materials synthesis, result validation, and experimental optimization. This process heavily depends on researchers’ efforts and experience while simultaneously constraining innovation possibilities due to insufficient
systematic knowledge integration. In contrast, the MARS-assisted design cycle accelerates this process significantly and enables efficient material design through knowledge-driven and
collaborative operations of multiple agents (G1–G5), compressing the entire procedure to just a few hours.
While precise wavelength control demonstrates the optimization capabilities, the fundamental challenge limiting perovskite
nanocrystal applications is their poor stability. Lead halide perovskites like CsPbBr3 possess ionic crystal structures that
readily decompose upon water exposure, causing complete
fluorescence quenching within hours. This weakness prevents
practical deployment in photovoltaics, LEDs, and other optoelectrical applications. According to Web of Science analysis
(see Figure S7), research on perovskite water stability has grown
exponentially, reflecting the urgent need for effective stabilization strategies. Given this critical challenge, we tasked MARS
with designing water-stable perovskite nanocrystals to showcase its capability in addressing pressing materials science
problems.
The multi-round interaction process illustrated in Figure 4B reveals how the MARS progresses from mechanistic understanding to creative design through increasingly refined problem analysis and solution strategies. In round 1, when researchers
inquired about core-shell stabilization mechanisms, MARS provided comprehensive explanations of how this approach protects perovskites through moisture barriers, defect passivation,
thermal stability enhancement, and ion migration suppression.
During this exploration of stability solutions, researchers noted
an additional opportunity revealed by literature analysis: the
growing interest in biomedical applications of perovskite materials (see Figure S8). These applications require both stability
and biocompatibility as essential properties. This insight guided
the subsequent interaction in round 2, where researchers introduced biocompatibility as an additional requirement. MARS
identified this dual challenge and proposed multiple candidate
solutions, including oxides, polymers, and biomimetic shells,
each with distinct advantages for different application scenarios.

To investigate the behavior of CsPbBr3@LP/PEG NCs in an
aqueous environment, the evolution of their photoluminescence
(PL) was tracked. As detailed in Figure S9A, the PL emission exhibited an initial slow increase followed by a gradual decrease
upon water immersion. This fluorescence evolution is not uncommon and has been observed in other phospholipid-coated
CsPbX3 systems.53 Previous studies have demonstrated that interactions with water can lead to surface defect passivation and
induce a phase transition from CsPbBr3 to CsPb2Br5, forming
heterostructures that initially enhance fluorescence.54,55 Our
X-ray crystallography (XRD) characterization confirmed the formation of the CsPb2Br5 phase during water immersion, consistent with these literature reports (see Figure S9B). Cytotoxicity
testing confirmed the effectiveness of phospholipid encapsulation in enhancing perovskite biocompatibility. In the evaluation,
CsPbBr3@LP/PEG maintained >95% cell viability at concentrations up to 100 μg/mL, while pristine CsPbBr3 showed significant
cytotoxicity (see Figure S10). These results demonstrate that
phospholipid encapsulation effectively prevents lead ion leaching
while improving optical stability for further biological applications.
Performance evaluation of the MARS framework
The capabilities of MARS were rigorously assessed through pairwise win rate based on established LLM evaluation frameworks
(see Note S11).56 Specifically, a benchmark dataset comprising
929 materials science text fragments with standardized test
questions was constructed through an automated questionanswer pair generation protocol. Claude3.5-Sonnet was employed to conduct iterative pairwise comparisons between
model responses, with randomized response ordering to mitigate positional bias.57 This method significantly enhances the
measurability of subtle differences between models, ensuring
robust discriminative validity and statistical reliability.
As shown in Figure 5A, the fundamental architectural advantage of MARS is demonstrated through systematic comparisons

using identical base models. When GPT-4o is employed as the
foundation in both systems, MARS achieves a 60.02% win rate
against the single-agent implementation. This significant
performance reveals critical architectural differences: MARS
overcomes the limitations of single-agent systems through
hierarchical design, where performance typically degrades
when processing extensive contexts and managing multiple
simultaneous tasks. By implementing specialized agents with
defined responsibilities and enabling multi-round, depth-focused
information retrieval within isolated task groups, MARS effectively reduces cognitive burden on individual models while
enhancing overall system stability and knowledge integration capabilities.36 Additionally, MARS also outperforms ChatGPT-4o
(the web interface version), achieving a 61.89% win rate. This
result demonstrates how domain-specific knowledge integration
and hierarchical multi-agent architecture provide advantages
over even highly optimized consumer AI systems.
Figure 5B demonstrates the adaptability of MARS across
different foundation models, validating the broad applicability of
the framework. Beyond the closed-source GPT-4o accessed via
API, MARS was successfully implemented using DeepSeek-R1,
a powerful open-source model with strong reasoning capabilities.
The pairwise comparison results show that MARS (R1) consistently outperforms both the single-agent (R1) and the standalone
DeepSeek-R1 model, confirming that the multi-agent architecture
provides performance advantages regardless of foundation
models. The average win rate analysis in Figure 5C reveals that
MARS systems achieve higher overall performance compared to
their single-agent counterparts and base models. The overall
win rates were calculated by averaging win rates from all unique,
non-self pairwise comparisons (see Figure S11). The performance
hierarchy clearly shows that MARS benefits from stronger foundation models, with the R1-based implementation achieving higher
average win rates than the GPT-4o version. This dependence on
base model capabilities suggests that as LLMs continue to
improve, the MARS framework will correspondingly enhance its
performance in materials science applications. The multi-agent architecture thus provides a scalable approach that can adapt to
and benefit from ongoing advances in LLMs.
While automated benchmarking provides quantitative comparisons, validating the practical utility of MARS in domainspecific scenarios requires expert assessment. Our evaluation
panel comprised 10 researchers with extensive expertise in
nanomaterial synthesis: 3 PhD candidates, 3 assistant researchers, 2 associate researchers, and 2 professors. All evaluations followed a double-blind protocol, with each test case
independently scored by 5 experts. We employed a 10-point
comprehensive scoring system evaluating three primary dimensions: scientific accuracy (40%), task completion (30%), and
reasoning quality (30%). The evaluation tasks spanned four
key aspects of materials research: structure design, synthesis
protocol development, property prediction, and application optimization. Detailed results and task-specific performance analyses are provided in Note S9, where MARS consistently demonstrated superior accuracy and experimental actionability
compared to baseline models (Figure S12).

DISCUSSION
This work introduces MARS, an LLM-driven platform for autonomous materials development. MARS integrates 19 specialized
agents within a hierarchical framework, which are organized
into functional groups to facilitate collaborative execution of
complex tasks spanning design, synthesis, and optimization.
By leveraging a curated knowledge base and a hybrid RAG
approach, MARS achieves enhanced accuracy and mitigates
LLM hallucinations, enabling professional-grade, autonomous
experimentation. Significantly, MARS achieved rapid optimization of material properties (e.g., tuning green-emitting perovskite
nanocrystals to blue emission within 10 iterations) and discovery
of novel materials within hours, significantly accelerating the

ll
Article
research process. Beyond efficiency gains, the MARS architecture enables seamless information flow and knowledge accumulation throughout the research pipeline, creating a responsive
closed-loop system that adapts to experimental feedback in
real time.
MARS represents a significant step toward autonomous materials research. This system reduces substantial repetitive work,
allowing researchers to focus on creative thinking and scientific
insights. The flexible, collaborative agent framework permits
customization by researchers, enabling integration of local databases and adaptation to diverse experimental platforms. Such
features facilitate tailoring to specific research needs while expanding applicability across various materials and synthesis
techniques. Despite these advantages, current limitations must
be acknowledged. MARS primarily relies on existing literature
knowledge for combinatorial optimization, with its innovative capacity currently limited to incremental improvements rather than
breakthrough discoveries. This reflects fundamental challenges
in applying LLMs to scientific research: distinguishing rational
inference from hallucinatory outputs and balancing knowledge
dependence with exploratory innovation. The system functions
as an intelligent research assistant that accelerates discovery
cycles rather than replacing human creativity.
Beyond performance, reproducibility and runtime error
handling represent critical factors for practical adoption in laboratory settings. The MARS architecture addresses reproducibility through a comprehensive open-source implementation
with standardized interfaces between agent modules and robotic components. This design allows other laboratories to replicate system functionality, though some adaptation to specific
hardware configurations remains necessary. Regarding error
management, MARS employs a multi-tiered approach to handle
failures in both design and synthesis processes: human intervention is strategically integrated at critical decision points, autonomous replanning occurs when agents detect suboptimal outcomes, and rule-based verification protects robotic execution.
This flexible error handling capability ensures experimental continuity despite the unpredictable challenges inherent in materials
research, maintaining system reliability without constant expert
supervision.
Future development will focus on standardized interfaces for
laboratory equipment, enabling more complex experimental
control. Beyond predefined functions, future LLMs could autonomously devise experimental strategies, optimize parameters,
and adapt to unexpected situations, realizing embodied intelligence in materials research. Besides, while the hybrid RAG
approach mitigates hallucinations, further advancements in
LLM architectures and domain-specific fine-tuning are crucial.
Critical directions include integrating physics-based simulations
and first-principles calculations to enhance scientific reasoning
capabilities, developing more reliable metrics for assessing innovation versus hallucination. We believe the evolution from knowledge-driven to genuinely autonomous innovation represents the
necessary trajectory for AI in scientific research, with the current
work establishing an important foundation for this long-term
goal. Future MARS iterations promise to accelerate materials
discovery and development to meet evolving scientific and industrial demands.

METHODS
Architecture details
MARS design
The MARS system utilizes GPT-4o from OpenAI as its foundation
model, with the temperature parameter set to 0.1 to ensure high
stability and consistency during task execution. The AutoGen
framework (https://github.com/microsoft/autogen) simplifies
complex LLM workflow orchestration, automation, and optimization through multi-agent dialogue mechanisms. Its flexible architecture enables developers to customize agent behaviors and
interaction patterns according to specific task requirements.
AutoGen provides two core agent types: AssistantAgent and
UserProxyAgent. In this study, we utilize AssistantAgent provided by AutoGen, defining agent roles and functions through
system_message and tools parameters. Simultaneously, we
set up UserProxyAgent as a human control interface to implement human-in-the-loop in the multi-agent system, enabling human intervention at critical points during task execution. In terms
of agent orchestration, MARS primarily employs two modes:
Selector Group Chat and Swarm (https://github.com/openai/
swarm). Selector Group Chat is a multi-agent dialogue mode
based on model selection mechanisms, where agents broadcast
messages to other members in turns, and an Orchestrator agent
selects the next speaking agent or team based on conversation
context. The Swarm mode is a multi-agent collaboration mode
based on task delegation mechanisms. Agents dynamically
delegate tasks to more suitable agents based on task requirements and capabilities, ensuring efficient task allocation and
execution within the team. The core mechanism of Swarm relies
on agents generating HandoffMessages to specify task delegation targets. The admin agents between teams coordinate
through the Selector Group Chat mode, while task allocation
within teams relies on the Swarm mode. Further details
regarding the methods can be found in the supplemental
information.
SCIENTIST group
The scientist team consists of four materials science agents,
each initialized through AssistantAgent. We guide different scientist agents to form different capability preferences through
system prompts. Within the scientist team, we use AutoGen’s
Swarm mode to orchestrate agents, with four scientist agents
communicating directly with the Scientist Team Admin agent
through task submission.
Knowledge base and retrieval tools
We established a knowledge base containing over 100,000
nano-material-related literature papers and developed interfaces based on keyword retrieval and vector retrieval for efficient
research material access. Specifically, we used the MinerU tool
to convert literature PDFs to markdown format for an efficient
knowledge base. For the SCIENTIST group, we customized
the following retrieval tools. Vector database and search: to optimize the RAG system’s retrieval, we segmented papers by section and vectorized text segments using the gte-Qwen2-1.5Binstruct model (https://huggingface.co/thenlper/gte-large). The
vectors are stored in a Weaviate database of Dify, with API functionalized as hybird_retrieval_from_knowledge_base providing

keywords and vectors. Knowledge graph: based on the knowledge base, we classified literature by research topics and utilized GraphRAG to construct knowledge graphs for different
research topics, with corresponding interfaces available through

WebSearch tools based on the Bing search engine and

ENGINEER group
The MARS engineer agent team consists of four agents defined
based on AutoGen’s AssistantAgent class, coordinating through
system prompts and tools. Within the team, we employ
AutoGen’s Swarm mode to organize engineer agents and admin
agents, efficiently completing subtasks distributed by the Orchestrator through task submission. The following tools support
the ENGINEER group. Code Sandbox Environment: AutoGen
provides DockerCommandLineCodeExecutor, which relies on
Docker container isolation environments for code execution that
might cause conflicts or crashes in local Python environments.
Python REPL: we directly use PythonCodeExecutionTool to
execute Python code reviewed and confirmed safe by the code
reviewer. Object-based storage: we introduced object storage
tools using MinIO to build a local object storage service, encapsulated through FastAPI, to maintain the integrity and stability of
structured experimental schemes.
EXECUTOR group
To address potential process blocking during long wait times
for characterization data collection, data collection tools are
set with a 6-h execution time and a 30-s sleep mechanism
when data are unavailable. We customized the following tools
for the EXECUTOR group. Mass unit conversion implemented
the mol2mg tool based on the periodictable Python package
for converting molar mass to weight units. Scheme transmission API: implemented using FastAPI for transmitting structured synthesis scheme access links directly to robots or island
stations. Results obtain API: implemented using FastAPI for
direct access to characterization device data. Camera detector
provides IP Camera video stream interface based on RTSP protocol for real-time monitoring of mobile robots and island
stations.

Cell viability assessment

cultured for 24 h. Cells were then incubated with various concentrations (0–100 μg/mL) of pristine CsPbBr3 or CsPbBr3@LP/PEG
for 24 h. CCK-8 reagent was added, and absorbance was

Viable and dead cell staining

Note S1: Code implementation
1.1 Core code of MARS
The core code of MARS is a multi-agent system implemented based on the Autogen[S1] framework,
which provides flexible and efficient tools for building complex multi-agent collaborations. The core
code is located in the backend folder of the Github Repo containing implementations of key modules
such as multi-agent definitions, RAG construction, ELO comparison agents, and GraphRAG [S2]. These
modules collectively form a complete multi-agent backend system that supports end-to-end operations
from task planning to execution.
1.2 Tools
All self-implemented tool code can be found in the backend/tools.py file of the GitHub repository, while
the tool server code based on FastAPI(https://fastapi.tiangolo.com/) and Dify workflow (https://dify.ai).
It should be noted that some tools are not self-implemented, including: Code Sandbox, Python REPL,
Matplotlib, Optuna, Matminer, and Scikit. Among them, the four libraries
Matplotlib(https://matplotlib.org/), Optuna(https://optuna.org/),
Matminer(https://hackingmaterials.lbl.gov/matminer/), and Scikit(https://scikit-learn.org/) are installed
as additional Python packages in the Python REPL environment.
1.3 Message passing
We utilized the AutoGen framework to orchestrate this multi-agent system, defining two primary
message-passing mechanisms:
1. Forward Context: Except for the task planning department, the messages received by other
departments include system messages, instructions, and context. System messages are predefined,
while the task planning department is responsible for assigning instructions, and the messages
exchanged between other departments are handled as contextual information.
2. Feedback Information: Message passing between departments heavily relies on contextual
information. When the feedback optimization department proposes optimization suggestions, the
information is passed backward. Additionally, the feedback mechanism requires the knowledge
retrieval module to persistently store experimental logs, regardless of the success or failure of the
experiments.
When a user issues an instruction regarding automated material synthesis research, the task planning
department comprehensively coordinates the activities of all agent departments. Specifically, the task
planning department is responsible for sending instructions to other departments and receiving their
responses. Upon receiving instructions from the task planning department, each agent department
processes the task and responds to both the task planning department and the next relevant agent
department. The complete automated material synthesis research process requires close collaboration
among all agent departments to ensure efficient completion of the workflow.

Note S2: Web application
To better demonstrate the process of multi-agent task completion in the MARS system, we developed
a Web demonstration application based on Vue.js. This application not only provides an intuitive user
interface but also integrates rich functional modules, including task progress visualization, agent status
monitoring, and history query. Users can access the application via a browser to view the real-time
operation of the multi-agent system.
Additionally, to enhance user experience, we integrated two network cameras based on the RTSP
protocol into the Web application for real-time observation of robot actions. By embedding video
streams, users can more clearly understand the performance of robots in real-world scenarios and
analyze task execution results in conjunction with system logs. The relevant code for the Web
application is located in the frontend folder of the Github Repo, which includes the design of front-end
pages, API call logic, and implementation details of video stream processing.

Note S3: Knowledge database construction
3.1 Data collection
(1) Academic paper collection
A high-quality academic paper database was assembled to construct our knowledge base,
focusing on materials systems relevant to our laboratory research (nanomaterials, such as perovskites
and gold nanorods). Three complementary approaches were employed:
i. Literature collections accumulated by our research team over years of study, including PDF
documents managed through the Zotero reference management system;
ii. Systematic expansion of the database through Web of Science searches using keywords and
seminal papers recommended by team members with domain expertise, followed by batch downloads
from high-impact journals through institutional library subscriptions. This combined approach resulted
in a comprehensive local database containing approximately 2,000 materials science publications.
Due to copyright restrictions, the full-text documents or vectorized database content cannot be
publicly shared on platforms like GitHub.
(2) Book resources
Authoritative materials science textbooks and monographs were incorporated to complement the
specialized knowledge from research papers with foundational theories and systematic concepts.
These were selected based on recommendations from experienced laboratory members, including
classics such as "Nanomaterials: Synthesis, Properties and Applications" and "Perovskite Materials Synthesis, Characterisation, Properties and Applications". These resources were legally obtained
through institutional access to electronic book databases via our university and the Chinese Academy
of Sciences libraries.
(3) Web resource integration
Recognizing that a static local knowledge base cannot fully cover all emerging research areas, a
dynamic online retrieval mechanism was implemented. The system uses search engines like Bing to
generate relevant queries based on user questions, retrieving links primarily to authoritative journal
websites. Through institutional IP authentication, these pages are accessed using the library to
extract displayed abstracts, figure captions, and key paragraphs in compliance with the publisher's
terms of service. Importantly, large-scale web scraping or crawling operations are not performed.
Instead, relevant content is accessed dynamically by MARS on demand during runtime, ensuring
compliance with publisher policies while maintaining system responsiveness.
3.2 Corpus preprocessing
Specifically, to support the development of the hybrid RAG system, we compiled approximately 10,000
academic papers, several classic books, and web snapshots accumulated by our research group over
the past decades, serving as the core data foundation for the system. To construct a corpus that is easy
to process, we designed a data cleaning pipeline to uniformly convert all documents into a format
suitable for knowledge base construction. The specific process is as follows:
(1) PDF standardization: First, we converted all documents, books, and web snapshots from various
sources into PDF format to ensure consistency in subsequent processing.
(2) Markdown format conversion: Next, we used the MinerU[S3] tool to convert all PDF documents into
Markdown format. MinerU not only extracts text from the documents but also automatically crops images
and tables from the documents, replacing them with unique URL links. The benefit of this operation is
that all documents are transformed into pure text corpus, facilitating subsequent processing.
(3) Document natural segmentation: The traditional method for constructing knowledge bases uses
fixed-length text segmentation with overlaps between segments, which has been proven to be less
effective. Thanks to the well-organized structure of scientific research papers, we adopt an advanced
approach that combines natural segmentation with parent-child retrieval to construct the knowledge
base. Specifically, for each paper, we use the heading symbol "#" in Markdown syntax to split the
document, obtaining M natural chunks. Then, we treat each natural chunk as a parent text block and
further divide it into N child sentences using the newline character "\n". These child sentences serve as
indexes for the parent text block. The advantage of this approach is that it maximally preserves semantic
continuity through natural segmentation while ensuring semantic coherence in retrieval results through
parent-child retrieval.
3.3 Database establishment
After completing the document format conversion, we needed to further transform the document
segments into structured data suitable for retrieval and inference. To achieve this, we processed each
text chunk as follows:
(1) Embedding generation: We first fed the text chunks into the gte-Qwen2-1.5B-instruct[S4] embedding

model, which can handle text chunks of up to 32K in length, generating corresponding text embeddings.
These embeddings provide the basis for subsequent vector retrieval.
(2) Keyword extraction: At the same time, we used a keyword extraction algorithm to identify important
keywords from the text, which are used to optimize and assist the BM25 algorithm retrieval (as shown
in Equation 1).

Note S4: An instance of hybrid retrieval result
4.1 Hybrid retrieval process
When a scientist agent receive a sub-task from Admin of Scientist group, and the scientist agent call the
tool of hybrid retrieval, the hybrid RAG system processes the input through embedding, keyword, and
knowledge graph. The specific steps are as follows:
(1) Similarity retrieval: The embedding of the agent input is first sent to the weaviate vector database
for similarity retrieval, filtering out multiple text segments with similarity scores greater than 0.5. Similarity
retrieval generally uses cosine similarity.
(2) Keyword retrieval: Next, the system uses the BM25 algorithm to input the keywords from the agent
query into the vector database, again filtering out multiple text segments with similarity scores greater
than 0.5.
(3) Weight calculation and filtering: To integrate the results from both retrieval methods, we assign
weights of 0.7 and 0.3 to the results from embedding-based retrieval and keyword-based retrieval,
respectively. Then, we calculate the weighted average score for the retrieved text segments and filter
out multiple document segments with a weighted score greater than 0.7. Since there is no standard
value for retrieval weights in the implementation of RAG, all values mentioned in this article are manually
selected through testing.
Although the document segments with high similarity scores are somewhat correlation to the agent's
query, to further enhance the accuracy of the retrieval results, we introduced a rerank model (gtemultilingual-reranker-base[S5]) to reorder these document segments. The purpose of this step is to
ensure that the retrieved document segments are not only similar but also highly relevant to the agent's
specific query. And then, the system selects the top 10 document segments with the highest similarity
and correlation as the optimal retrieval results. It is worth noting that the similarity scores and weight
values are empirically determined and may need adjustment according to the content of different
knowledge bases during actual use.
For the document segments retrieved based on embeddings and keywords, they often originate from
different documents, which may lead to a lack of a holistic knowledge perspective when answering agent
queries. To address this issue, GraphRAG constructs a unified knowledge graph for all documents and
performs a global search based on the agent's query, generating a topic-level response (called
community report in GraphRAG). This knowledge segment acts as a supplement to the vector retrieval
results, providing a more comprehensive perspective and contextual support. Finally, we return the
document segments along with the community reports generated by GraphRAG as retrieval results.

6.1 Details of robotic platform
Our second-generation experimental robotic platform evolved from the first-generation robotic scientist
platform, showcasing its capabilities in micro/nano materials synthesis. This experimental robotic
platform represents a significant advancement in the integration of multi-agent systems and automated
experimental control.
The central element is the automated platform workstation (1), which acts as the “brain” of the platform.
This workstation is responsible for sending instructions to the various devices on the platform and
executing the automated synthesis process. The platform includes one independent mobile robot (2),
which is controlled by the workstation and primarily responsible for the preparation and transportation
of reagents and consumables. The reagents and consumables are stored in a designated consumables
management area (3), which holds both untreated and treated reagent bottles. A key feature of the
platform is the mobile robot (4), also controlled by the workstation, which executes a wide range of
experimental procedures within the platform. It performs critical tasks such as transporting reagents to
different sections of the platform. The high-precision reagent adding device (5) enables the automated
addition of powdered or liquid reagents. These are further processed at the liquid workstation (6), where
tasks like pipetting, stirring, and heating reactions are carried out. After reactions are completed, the
mobile robot transfers the samples for analysis. The platform is equipped with two analytical devices,
including a fluorescence spectrometer (7) for fluorescence detection, and a UV spectrometer (8) for
ultraviolet detection and analysis. Additionally, the platform features a 12-channel reaction device (9) for
simultaneous multi-sample processing, and a concentration measuring device (10) that determines
reagent concentration levels. To handle larger solution volumes, the platform incorporates a solution
dispensing device (11) for pre-treatment and dispensing. Figure 10b shows a photograph of the entire
experimental platform, where all these devices are integrated into a large-scale experimental system.
The mobile robot (4) demonstrates the programmability of the system, capable of executing various
complex experimental workflows. Figures 10c and 10d present front and top views of the structure of
the platform, offering a detailed visualization of the equipment layout.
6.2 Unified control middleware
The unified control middleware software is the central part of the entire system. It is an upper-level
control software for workstations and mobile robots implemented in C#. This software is responsible
for managing the low-level control logic of robotic devices and interacting with multi-agent systems
through standardized interfaces. To achieve more efficient task scheduling and real-time monitoring,
we extended this software by adding an HTTP Server to listen for experimental process instructions
sent by agents within the MARS system.
This portion of the code resides in the "middleware" folder of the Github repository and includes the
implementation of the HTTP Server, drivers for communicating with robot hardware, and a task queue
management module. Through this middleware layer, users can easily integrate different robotic
devices into the system while supporting dynamic adjustment of task priorities and providing real-time
feedback on execution status.

9.1 Automated evaluation
Automated evaluation plays a crucial role in assessing the quality of question-answer (QA) pairs
generated for Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems. This section outlines the process of
automatically constructing an evaluation dataset, which serves as the foundation for benchmarking
different agents.
9.1.1 Automatically build evaluation dataset
The construction of the evaluation dataset is performed in two primary stages: QA Pair Generation and
QA Pair Critique. These stages ensure that the dataset contains high-quality QA pairs suitable for
evaluating RAG systems.
To construct this dataset, we first randomly sampled over 100 papers from a repository of literature
with IF>8 and extracted text chunks, then retained only those chunks with sufficient context—typically
containing more than 150 tokens. These text chunks were then processed by language models like
o3-mini to generate QA pairs, using predefined prompt templates to create questions, answers, topics,
and metadata linked to the source documents. To efficiently handle large-scale datasets, parallel
processing techniques were employed, enabling the generation of thousands of QA pairs in a timeeffective manner.
We also implemented an automated review process for the generated QA pairs to ensure their quality.
This involved evaluating each QA pair based on three key criteria: groundedness, relevance, and
standalone quality. Groundedness assesses whether the question is well-supported by the provided
context, while relevance measures how pertinent the question is to the topic. Standalone quality
evaluates whether the question makes sense independently of the context. Each QA pair was
reviewed by a language model, scored on a 5-point scale, and given textual feedback for each
criterion. Only QA pairs scoring 4 or higher on all three criteria were retained, ensuring that the final
dataset contains high-quality entries suitable for robust evaluation.
The code related to this RAG validation dataset can be found in the backend/evaluate/eval_rag_data
folder of the Github Repo. Below is an example of this validation set:

Specifically, we selected several representative Agents (including gpt-4o-mini, gpt-4o, gpt-4o with CoT
and RAG, MARS, o3-mini, and Deepseek-R1) to answer the questions in the validation set.
Subsequently, we used the Claude3.5-Sonnet model as the evaluation model to vote on the answers
from any two Agents along with the reference answer, selecting the response that Claude3.5-Sonnet
deemed optimal.
To ensure fairness in the evaluation process and minimize potential biases, we introduced
anonymization and order randomization strategies in our experimental design. Specifically, all Agents'
responses were anonymized when submitted to Claude3.5-Sonnet for evaluation to avoid bias due to
model names or origins. Additionally, we eliminated inherent biases potentially introduced by attention
mechanisms by randomly swapping the order of the Agents' responses and averaging scores over
multiple experiments.
Finally, we tallied the number of wins, losses, and ties for each pair of Agents on the RAG validation
set. To more accurately reflect the relative performance between Agents, we excluded the impact of tie
votes and divided the number of wins by the total number of non-tie votes, thereby calculating one
Agent's win rate relative to another.

Note S10. Safety implications: risk mitigation and safety strategies for multi-agent material
scientists
The development of multi-agent LLM-driven systems in materials science, such as our Materials
Autonomous Research System, offers immense opportunities to accelerate scientific discovery and
streamline material innovation. However, these systems also introduce unique risks that must be
carefully managed to prevent unintended consequences, misuse, or improper application, particularly
by non-expert users. This section outlines specific risk and safety strategies tailored for multi-agent
systems, focusing on ensuring the responsible and secure use of AI in the field of materials science.
The primary risk mitigation strategy for multi-agent systems is to establish robust coordination and
communication protocols among the agents, alongside strict oversight by human operators. It is crucial
that domain experts—material scientists, ethicists, and policymakers—remain actively involved in
supervising the system's decision-making processes. Human oversight must be embedded in all critical
stages, from research design to validation, ensuring that AI agents act in a coordinated manner and
their decisions are vetted for safety and accuracy. This hybrid approach, combining multi-agent AI
capabilities with expert human oversight, helps mitigate the risk of erroneous conclusions and
inappropriate applications.
To address security concerns and dual-use implications, multi-agent systems are designed with built-in
checks to monitor agent interactions and outputs continuously. This includes developing early warning
systems to detect anomalies in agent behavior that could signal potential misuse or deviation from
intended research objectives. Controlled access to sensitive AI capabilities and implementing a rolebased hierarchy among agents can further limit the potential for misuse by ensuring that specific tasks
are only accessible to agents with defined responsibilities.
Integrating foresight and preventative measures into the multi-agent system development lifecycle is
crucial for mitigating risks. This involves defining clear operational boundaries for each agent, equipping
agents with the ability to recognize when they are approaching these boundaries, and raising alerts
when predefined thresholds are exceeded. Additionally, continuous monitoring and audit trails are
necessary to maintain transparency and traceability in agent actions, allowing human supervisors to
understand and rectify any unintended consequences.
Promoting transparency within multi-agent research environments is equally critical for responsible AI
development. Encouraging open science practices and fostering a community-driven approach helps to
ensure that the deployment and use of multi-agent systems are subjected to peer review and public
scrutiny. Feedback loops from the broader scientific community can help identify potential issues early,
mitigating risks before they escalate. Transparency in agent behavior and decision-making logic also
helps foster trust, as stakeholders can better understand the rationale behind each decision made by
the system.
Furthermore, targeted training programs for researchers and operators are recommended to address
ethical considerations, secure handling of AI-generated outputs, and recognize warning signs of misuse.
Emphasis should be placed on understanding how multi-agent interactions may lead to emergent
behaviors, both positive and potentially hazardous. Building expertise in managing and interpreting the
actions of multi-agent systems will empower users to maintain safe and effective control over these
advanced research tools.
The outlined risk mitigation strategies aim to ensure that multi-agent AI systems like MARS evolve in
ways that maximize their positive impact on material discovery while minimizing associated risks. By
maintaining strict oversight, promoting transparency, and encouraging a culture of responsibility, we
believe the safe deployment of AI technologies in scientific research can be achieved. Further
exploration of these strategies, including specific risk assessment protocols and practical risk mitigation
measures, is detailed in this Supplementary Information section to guide the responsible development
of multi-agent AI systems in materials science.
